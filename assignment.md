# Report Agent Behaviors

## Summary

You will create a report that documents and analyzes agent behaviors in the historical world you built by adapting Stanford’s Smallville style simulation. Your goal is to examine how agents behave within your chosen historical setting, how the world design shapes those behaviors, and what the simulation reveals about social interaction, routine, memory, goals, and historical plausibility.

For example, you might create a historical world such as a Qing dynasty market town and a Depression era American neighborhood. You will then observe what agents do in that world and analyze whether their behaviors make sense in relation to the historical context you designed.

Your report should clearly show:

- the historical world you created
- the agents and their roles in that world
- the behaviors and interactions you observed
- the extent to which those behaviors were historically plausible
- how the environment, schedule, roles, and prompts may have shaped those behaviors
- what this means for using agent based simulations to study or teach history

## Your Task

Create a report that analyzes agent behaviors in your historical world.

You should:

- choose or describe your historical setting
- introduce the agents and their identities or roles
- observe and document agent behaviors over time
- analyze key interactions, routines, or events
- evaluate the historical plausibility of those behaviors
- discuss what your simulation helps reveal, and what it may distort or miss

## Historical World Focus

Choose one historical world that you or your group created or adapted.

Examples:

- a village during the Great Depression
- an ancient trading port

## Required Report Components

1. Historical World Overview

Briefly explain:

- the time period and location
- the key social, political, or cultural context
- the important places in your world
- why you designed the world this way

2. Agent Setup

Describe the agents in your world.

Include:

- agent identities
- social roles
- goals, routines, or relationships
- any background details that influence behavior

3. Behavior Documentation

Document what agents actually did.

Include at least 3 specific observations:

- routines
- interactions
- movement
- conversations
- unexpected events

Use screenshots, logs, or summaries.

4. Behavior Analysis

Analyze:

- patterns in behavior
- how environment, prompts, and roles shaped outcomes
- which behaviors felt realistic vs artificial
- what surprised you

5. Historical Plausibility Evaluation

Evaluate:

- whether behaviors fit the historical context
- whether roles and interactions make sense
- whether there are anachronisms or distortions
- what seems missing or oversimplified

6. Simulation, Context, and Critique

As discussed in class, the simulation is **only one part of a larger learning process**, not a complete representation of history.

In this section, you must explicitly address:

- What prior knowledge, readings, or context would students need **before** engaging with this simulation?
- Why should the simulation **not** be used as a standalone way to learn this history?
- What assumptions does your simulation make about the historical context?
- What does the simulation get wrong, flatten, or fail to represent?
- How would you design surrounding learning activities (before or after) to help students **critically interpret** the simulation?

You should demonstrate a clear understanding that:

- simulations generate **possible behaviors, not historical truth**
- historical learning must involve **context, interpretation, and critique**
- students should engage with the simulation as something to **question and analyze**, not just observe

7. Strengths and Limitations

Discuss:

- what the simulation helps reveal
- what it cannot capture well
- whether it is more useful for engagement, exploration, or critique

8. Final Reflection

Answer:

What did analyzing agent behavior in a historical world help you understand about both history and AI systems?

## Minimum Requirements Checklist

Your report must include:

- one clearly defined historical world
- agent descriptions
- at least 3 behavior observations
- behavior analysis
- historical plausibility evaluation
- explicit critique of simulation as a learning tool
- final reflection
